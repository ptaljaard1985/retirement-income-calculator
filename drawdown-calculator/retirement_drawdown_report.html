IV. Assumptions · what the projection rests on

The rules of this projection.

Parameter Value What this means, and why
Expected return — Nominal total return on both the living annuities and the discretionary portfolios, net of platform and advice fees. Reflects a moderate-risk, equity-tilted mandate held through retirement.
Inflation (CPI) — Your monthly need and annual lump sums escalate with CPI each year. This is the SARB's mid-range target — results assume a stable macro environment, not the tails.
Living annuity draw band 2.5 – 17.5% The FAIS-mandated range. Draws below 2.5% are not possible; above 17.5%, legislation caps you. The projection raises the draw rate as needed, and flags the age the ceiling binds.
Auto top-up — When the living-annuity draw alone won't meet the target need, the plan draws the shortfall from discretionary capital. Turning this off forces you to live on the LA band plus other income — useful to see where the shortfall begins.
Tax year 2026 / 27 Income tax modelled on the current SARS tables, including the over-65 and over-75 rebates. Bracket creep of 3% per year is applied to brackets, base amounts, rebates and the CGT exclusion to reflect Treasury's stated intent of partial fiscal-drag relief.
Longevity horizon 100 The projection runs to age 100 for the younger spouse. "Sustainable until age N" means the household stays at or above its target income through age N, whether capital remains or not.

V. The four levers · what you can change

If the horizon shortens, there are four ways to lengthen it.

i. Expenses
Lower the monthly target. A small reduction stretches the horizon meaningfully — the most durable lever, because every saved rand compounds for the rest of your life.
ii. Returns
Hold more equity; accept more volatility. Each extra percent of real return buys several years of horizon — but a bad sequence early in retirement can erase more than the upside ever gives back.
iii. Retirement age
Work one more year and two things improve: capital compounds untouched, and the drawdown window shortens. A single extra year at the peak of earnings typically adds two to three years to the horizon.
iv. Income
Add a durable stream: a rental, a part-time consulting book, a deferred pension. Even a modest non-portfolio income materially changes the shape of the plan — it protects capital from sequence risk in the early years.

The levers are not independent — pulling one almost always relieves pressure on the others. The purpose of the compare view in the calculator is to try them in combination, not in isolation.

The capital behind the income.

Living annuity balance Discretionary balance Withdrawal rate · right axis
nominal · left axis capital · right axis draw %

Early years favour discretionary capital — drawing from it defers tax and keeps the LA compounding. As the disc pot exhausts (around never), the plan leans harder on the LAs and the withdrawal rate climbs toward the 17.5% ceiling. Once the ceiling binds, income can't rise further without lifting the underlying capital — which is when the horizon starts to shorten.

XII. Methodology · how the projection is built

What the numbers mean, and don't.

What we model

For each year from retirement to age 100 of the younger spouse, the projection computes the household's required income in today's money, converts it to nominal rands at the year's CPI-adjusted level, then fills it from three sources: other income, a living-annuity draw inside the 2.5–17.5% band, and (if auto top-up is on) a discretionary draw to cover any shortfall. Both pots grow at the expected return, applied to the post-draw balance.

Sustainability, precisely

The horizon is the last age at which the three sources combined fully meet the target need. At that age plus one, the plan either depletes the living annuity, hits the draw ceiling, or runs the discretionary pot dry — the annotations on the charts mark which happens when.

What we don't model

Sequence-of-returns risk (early bad years carry outsized impact and are not captured by a single expected return). Estate duty, executors' fees, section 7C trust loans. Medical-aid step-ups above CPI. Once-off events other than those captured on the capital events ledger. Platform / advice fees beyond what's already netted off the return assumption.

What this report is, and isn't

It's a decision aid — a way to see whether a lifestyle and a capital base meet each other, and which lever to pull if they don't. It's not advice for your specific circumstances, and it's not a guarantee of outcomes. Markets, tax law, and life all move; the plan should be revisited at least annually.

i.
Expenses.
Lower the monthly target. A R 5 000 reduction from R 50 000 to R 45 000 stretches the horizon by roughly 4 – 6 years, depending on when it happens. The most durable lever — it compounds every year.
Typical effect + 4 – 6 yrs per R 5 000/mo
ii.
Returns.
Hold more equity, accept more volatility. Each +1% of real return buys about 3 – 5 years, but the risk is real — a bad sequence early in retirement can erase more than the upside gives back.
Typical effect + 3 – 5 yrs per +1% real return

Retirement age.

Typical effect + 2 – 3 yrs per extra working year

Other income.
